01Executive Summary

We built The Equity Gap, a deployed decision-support dashboard for a Treasury Board Secretariat (TBS) equity analyst in the Office of the Chief Human Resources Officer (OCHRO) — the federal body statutorily responsible for monitoring employment equity across the public service. Today that analyst opens four separate published tables (one per designated equity group: Women, Indigenous Peoples, Persons with Disabilities, Members of Visible Minorities), cross-references department names and percentages by hand, and eyeballs a ranking — a half-day process that does not scale across 72 departments. The dashboard answers the same question — which department×group combinations sit furthest below their workforce-availability (WFA) benchmark, and which way are they trending — in the two minutes the analyst actually has before a weekly sync.

The dashboard is built on two real government sources: Treasury Board Employment Equity representation data (BT1-28, 2021-22 → 2024-25, 576 department×group×year rows) and the 2024 Public Service Employee Survey (PSES) Employment Equity Derived Variable dataset, joined by a reproducible Python pipeline gated by a runnable validator, never hand-pasted. Its output surfaces as ranked triage (Explore), cross-cutting patterns (Compare, including a divergence lens), per-department subgroup detail (Subgroups), year-over-year movement (Track), a generated five-block briefing (Present), and a real, guardrailed conversational assistant (Ask) — every output a signal for human review, never an automated decision.

Claude Code built this end to end from a living specification (CLAUDE.md) that was rewritten and extended repeatedly as real defects and new evidence surfaced — not written once and forgotten. Five Claude Skills and three subagents govern validation, cautious language, question routing, trend interpretation, and briefing generation; a reproducible pipeline (a fixed-order set of Python scripts) computes every derived field. After the deployment week, an independent governance-critic agent — with no memory of how the app was built — attacked the live app against its own written promises. Ten of eleven directly testable claims held with concrete evidence; one real, low-severity defect (a missing robots.txt) was found, fixed, and verified; one apparent failure turned out, on investigation, to be a deliberate animation the critic's non-JS tooling misread as missing data. That triage record and the fix are documented in full in Deployment_Assessment.md.

Live dashboard: https://equity-gap-dashboard.vercel.app

04Opportunity and Creativity

The intended user: a TBS/OCHRO employment-equity analyst — the central function statutorily accountable for monitoring equity across the federal public service and reporting on it annually. A secondary user is a departmental EDI lead who reads their own department's rows against the service-wide picture.

The decision the dashboard supports: where OCHRO directs its limited oversight attention — which department×group combinations to surface in the annual report, raise with a deputy head, or prioritize for engagement in the coming year. OCHRO does not run hiring inside departments; its lever is oversight, not operations, and the dashboard is built around that lever specifically.

Why this decision matters: the data already exists, published annually as four separate flat tables with no ranked, filterable, cross-group view. A missed severe gap surfaces later in an audit or a minister's briefing instead of from the analyst's own triage — the dashboard's whole value is closing that two-minutes-versus-half-a-day gap.

Why a dashboard is useful here: the underlying comparison — 72 departments × 4 designated groups, each against its own benchmark, in two dimensions (representation and trend) — is exactly the kind of multi-dimensional ranking a flat table cannot show at a glance but a filterable, sortable dashboard can.

Avoiding "charts from available data": the project explicitly declined to build several things the source data made easy — a composite equity score across groups, service-wide subgroup/region/age breakdowns that don't exist at the department level, and a fabricated executive-pipeline view — because none of them sharpen the specific departmental-oversight decision the dashboard exists to serve. Each refusal is dated and reasoned in CLAUDE.md §3, and one was later reopened only when new evidence (a per-department, cross-validated data source) actually cleared the bar the earlier refusal set.

The dashboard helps an OCHRO equity analyst decide where to direct oversight attention by making the ranked, benchmarked distance-from-target visible across all 72 departments and 4 designated groups at once — something four separate published tables do not allow.

05Specification / CLAUDE.md

CLAUDE.md is the single file Claude Code re-reads at the start of every session in this project. It is not documentation written after the fact — it is the standard the agent is held to, and every major build decision in this project traces back to a line in it.

  • Objective: where OCHRO directs its limited oversight attention, stated as a "no-dataset sentence" that names the decision without naming a single column.
  • Scope: a "Shipped" list, an "Out of scope (do not build)" list, and a running, dated log of things "considered and deliberately not built" — several of which were later revisited and reasoned again as new evidence arrived (a teammate's cross-validated data source clearing a bar an earlier refusal had set).
  • Data: two sources, one reproducible pipeline, with explicit "do not guess" rules (suppression means blank never zero; the WFA benchmark was rebased in 2023-24 and a gap change across that boundary reflects the rebase, not hiring; self-identification undercounts, especially for Persons with Disabilities).
  • Success criteria: a runnable, independently re-derivable evaluation harness (eval/run_eval.py, 10/10 checks) plus a pinned, verified "gold example" oracle — not prose claims.
  • Evaluation: §2's "How the thresholds were determined" documents every judgment call (the priority-flag quartile, the severity bands, the suppression rule, the divergence margin) with its source and its status as policy-vs-convention.

How it changed as the project developed (full detail in the revision log at the end of §6): the gold example was replaced once a hand-picked value turned out not to exist in the real data; PSES coverage was corrected from a claimed five indicators to the four that are actually reproducible from source; the capabilities section was rewritten to describe what the app's skills and subagents actually do, rather than an aspirational runtime story; and, most recently, the user stories were reworded into the course's taught "As a X, I want to Y, so that Z" template and the capability-to-form map was added explicitly naming which of the five capability forms (native action, skill, subagent, connector, fixed pipeline) each part of the app uses.

   Tuesday morning before my weekly sync with the Director, I want to scan all 72

   of combinations that belong in this year's report or a deputy-head
   conversation* — without manually cross-referencing four separate published
   tables.
2. **Marc, departmental EDI lead (secondary).** *As a departmental EDI lead,
   ahead of my quarterly equity committee meeting, I want to see my department's
   gap and trend against the service-wide WFA benchmark alongside PSES
   experience context, so that I can brief my committee accurately without
   overstating what the data proves.*
3. **Priya, OCHRO equity analyst.** *As an OCHRO equity analyst, when a deputy
   head asks for a one-pager on a specific department-group, I want a five-block
   briefing (finding, evidence, caveat, review required, next action) generated
   in under a minute, so that I have defensible, guardrailed language ready
   instead of drafting it from scratch each time.*

**Interrogation of story #1** (sharpest — it carries the objective's "under two
minutes" claim and exercises the dashboard's primary view, Explore):
- She's on the hook for directing OCHRO's limited oversight attention to the right
  departments *before* the annual report ships — a missed severe gap surfaces later
  in an audit or a minister's briefing instead of from her own triage.
- Today, without this dashboard, she opens four separate flat tables (one per
  designated group), manually cross-references department names and percentages,
  and eyeballs a ranking — a process that doesn't scale across 72 departments and
  can't be done in under half a day.
- With it, she opens Explore, sorts by gap within a chosen group, and immediately
  sees priority-flagged rows with N, WFA, and severity — turning that half-day

## 1. Objective

**Primary user.** An employment-equity analyst/advisor in the Treasury Board
Secretariat's Office of the Chief Human Resources Officer (OCHRO) — the central
function statutorily accountable for monitoring employment equity across the federal
public service and reporting on it each year. (Secondary user: a *departmental* EDI
lead who reads their own rows against the service-wide picture.)

**Their lever is oversight, not operations.** OCHRO does not run hiring inside
departments — departments do. So the decision this dashboard changes is **where the
central function directs its limited attention**: which department × group
combinations to surface in the annual report, raise with a deputy head, or
prioritize for engagement and challenge in the coming year.

The data is published annually as four separate flat tables (one per designated
group) with no ranked, filterable, cross-group view. This dashboard answers in under
two minutes: **which departments, for which designated groups, are furthest below
their workforce-availability benchmark, and which way are they trending — so
attention goes where the signal is strongest.**

No-dataset sentence: *this lets a central employment-equity analyst triage 72
departments × 4 groups for where to direct oversight attention in two minutes, which
the four separate published tables do not allow.*

**Deliberate judgment — the benchmark.** Workforce availability (WFA) is the only
valid benchmark, but this dashboard uses the **service-wide** WFA for every
department, not departmental WFA (which reflects each department's occupational mix
and is not in the published cross-department data). That makes the ranking a
**triage signal, not a precise league table** — a heavily-operational department is
measured against the same bar as a policy shop. A high ranking means "look here,"
to be refined with departmental WFA before any conclusion. Combined with voluntary
self-identification (which undercounts, especially Persons with Disabilities), the
honest claim is *directional prioritization*, not exact placement.

**The output, in one line.** A deployed decision-support dashboard whose unit of
output is a **department × designated-group row** — representation % (with raw N),
the WFA benchmark, the signed gap, a severity band, a priority flag, year-over-year
movement, and PSES experience context — surfaced these ways: **ranked triage**
(Explore), **cross-cutting patterns** (Compare — compounding shortfalls + a
divergence lens), **subgroup detail** (Subgroups — per-department experience by
subgroup plus service-wide subgroup reference tables), **year-over-year** (Track),
and a generated **five-block briefing** (Present) — plus a grounded, guardrailed
**Q&A** (Ask). Every output is a signal for
human review; the project produces no decisions, no composite scores, and no causal
claims.

## 2. What good looks like

A good output row gives a policy lead everything needed to decide whether to act,
without leaving the screen.

**Gold example (verified — re-derived by `eval/run_eval.py`):**
> Royal Canadian Mounted Police · Persons with Disabilities · Representation **5.5%
> (N=590 of 10,822)** · WFA **12.0%** · Gap **−709** · Severity **Substantial** ·
> ⚑ Priority · Signal: *substantially below the workforce-availability benchmark;
> recommended focus: recruitment pipeline review, accessibility review.*

**Every figure must show:** the representation %, the raw N it came from, and the
WFA benchmark beside it. A percentage without its N, or a gap without its benchmark,
fails.

**Testable success criteria:** `eval/run_eval.py` re-derives every shown number and
passes 10/10 checks, including the oracle above; `edi-data-guard` passes on the real
data and blocks a broken file; the Ask assistant grounds every figure and refuses
causal/staffing requests (see `eval/results.md`).

**Must never produce:** causal claims ("caused by", "proves discrimination"); blame
or moral ranking of departments; any inference about individuals; automated hiring/
firing/staffing recommendations; a composite score averaging across the four groups
(each is benchmarked against a different labour market).

### How the thresholds were determined

- **Priority flag — data-driven, not asserted.** A row is flagged when its gap sits
  in the **bottom quartile within its own equity-group × fiscal-year cohort**. The
  cutoff is computed from the actual distribution (`eval/run_eval.py` re-derives it),
  so it adapts to each group — whose benchmarks and spreads differ — rather than
  imposing one arbitrary number across groups.
- **Severity bands — a documented interpretability scale.** Slight <2 / Moderate
  2–4.9 / Substantial 5–9.9 / Severe ≥10 **percentage points below WFA**. This is a
  fixed *communication* scale (like a heat scale) chosen for legibility, not a
  statistical claim; the cut points are shown on screen and are tunable in one place
  (`pipeline/build_dataset.py`). Distance is always in pp of representation below the
  benchmark, so a band means the same thing for every group.
- **Suppression — an external policy rule, not ours.** Small-population cells are
  suppressed per Statistics Canada / Employment-Equity publication policy; the
  dashboard shows them as suppressed, never zero, and `edi-data-guard` enforces it.
- **Divergence margin — a documented floor, ranking is the emphasis.** A department
  is flagged for divergence when it is at/above benchmark **and** ≥5 pp below the
  *same group's* public-service average on harassment or discrimination. 5 pp is a
  conservative "meaningfully below peer" floor; results are ranked by shortfall so
  the magnitude carries the signal, not the cutoff.

## 3. Scope

**Shipped:** department-level gap table for all four designated groups (Women,
Indigenous Peoples, Persons with Disabilities, Members of Visible Minorities),
filterable by group and ranked by gap; priority flag (bottom quartile of gap per
group × year); severity scale (Slight <2pp / Moderate 2–4.9 / Substantial 5–9.9 /
Severe ≥10); WFA and raw N beside every representation figure; multi-year
representation trends over up to four years (2021-22 → 2024-25) with the 2023-24
benchmark rebase marked and gaps never compared across it;
a cross-group compare view (compounding shortfalls **plus a divergence lens** —
representation met but experience below the peer-group average); a dedicated
**Subgroups** tab (per-department subgroup workplace-experience across 6 themes
and 3 survey cycles, plus service-wide subgroup/salary/age/WFA-history reference
tables, with bar-chart visualizations and computed within-dataset signals); a
five-block briefing generator; a real, guardrailed conversational Ask tab; a
permanently visible responsible-use caveat.

**Out of scope (do not build):** individual-level data; causal/regression analysis;
automated policy or staffing recommendations; any view that collapses the groups
into one scale.

**Considered and deliberately not built (recorded decision, 2026-07-02).**
Service-wide **subgroup** breakdowns (racialized subgroups, Indigenous subgroups),
**region-of-work**, and **age-distribution** tables. They exist in the published
source (and a parallel build surfaces them), but only at the *service-wide* level —
they are **not per-department**, so they do not serve this dashboard's decision,
which is directing *departmental* oversight attention. Adding them would widen the
surface without sharpening the decision — the "clever build with no clear decision"
failure mode. **Multi-year representation trends** — which *do* serve the decision —
were subsequently built (2021-22 → 2024-25; see §4). The **executive-pipeline gap**
(overall vs. leadership representation) would also serve it, but the source publishes
executive representation only **service-wide**, not per department, so it cannot be
built at this dashboard's per-department grain and stays out of scope until
per-department executive data exists.

**Revisiting the 2026-07-02 decision (2026-07-03).** That decision declined the
parallel build's extra dimensions because they were **service-wide-only** and
therefore didn't sharpen the departmental-oversight decision. Its PSES
**workplace-experience** data (not representation) — subgroup breakdowns (Black,
South Asian, East Asian, Arab, First Nations, Métis, Inuit, Cognitive, Mental
health, Seeing) across 6 themes and 3 survey cycles (2020/2022/2024) — is
**per-department** (53 of our 72), which is the specific bar the service-wide data
didn't clear. Before merging, its representation data was cross-validated against
our own verified `equity.json`: **195 of 195 comparable department × group pairs
for 2024-25 matched within 0.3 percentage points.** That gives confidence the
source pipeline is genuine, though the experience scores themselves cannot be
independently re-derived by our own pipeline (the source's raw 2020/2022 PSES
microdata, demographic-recoding concordance, and department-name mapping files
aren't available to us) — shown in Explore's row detail (harassment only) and,
in full 6-theme detail with a department/group picker, on the dedicated
**Subgroups** tab (2026-07-04) — both labeled as a cross-validated external
source, not our own verified figure. **Subgroup
representation/WFA remains out of scope** — it does not exist at the department
level in any available source (confirmed by the source's own subgroup
representation field always being empty). See `Deployment_Log.md` for the full
cross-validation record.

## 4. Data

**Two sources, one reproducible pipeline** (`pipeline/`):
- **Representation & benchmark** — Treasury Board Employment Equity data, 2023-24
  and 2024-25. The input of record is `Knowledge/EMPLYOMENT EQUITY-TBS/knowledge/
  bt1_28_representation.csv` (283 rows, FY2021-22 → FY2024-25, wide format — one
  row per department per year), reshaped into the long-format canonical CSV
  `pipeline/extract_bt1_28_representation.py` produces and `edi-data-guard`
  validates: 576 rows = 288 (2023-24, 72 depts × 4) + 288 (2024-25, 72 depts × 4);
  77 suppressed. (2026-07-04: expanded from an earlier 35/71-department extract of
  the same source family to this source's full department coverage — see
  `Deployment_Log.md`.) A **four-year series** (2021-22 → 2024-25,
  `pipeline/build_history.py`, same source file) feeds the Track view: verified
  identical to the canonical data on the two overlap years (536/536 keys, 0
  mismatches), gated by `edi-data-guard`, and stitched across years **only where a
  department is unambiguously the same entity** — the RCMP's reported population
  changed across years (Civilian Staff → full force), so it is
  not stitched and stays current-year only.
- **Employee experience** — the **2024 Public Service Employee Survey**, Employment
  Equity Derived Variable dataset (`EEINFODV.csv`, fetched from open.canada.ca).
  `pipeline/extract_pses.py` reads the raw microdata; `pipeline/build_dataset.py`
  joins it, gated by `edi-data-guard`, and emits `web/src/data/equity.json`.

**PSES — four indicators (method documented in `web/src/data/meta.json`):**
engagement, diversity & inclusion, harassment, discrimination. Each subindicator
score is the mean of its **non-suppressed** SCORE100 questions at the department ×
equity-group level. **Mobility & retention is omitted** — its questions are
suppressed (code 9999) at this breakdown, so it cannot be computed and is not
fabricated. PSES is **context only**: never combined with representation in a
formula, never used to rank.

**Do not guess:** WFA is the only valid benchmark (service-wide values per group ×
year, in `meta.json` and `edi-data-guard`'s `references/columns.json`); blank means
suppressed for privacy, never zero; self-identification is voluntary, so groups —
especially Persons with Disabilities — may be undercounted; trends only for
departments present in both years; two data points are not a trend. **WFA is
periodically rebased**: the availability benchmark was substantially updated in
2023-24 (Persons with Disabilities 9.2 → 12.0, Members of Visible Minorities
17.3 → 22.7). Across that boundary a *gap* change reflects the rebase, not hiring —
the Track view marks it and never classifies a cross-boundary change as improvement;
representation % (members/all) is benchmark-independent and is the clean trend.

## 5. Capabilities (the agentic workspace)

Five capabilities are installed as **real Claude Code skills** under
`.claude/skills/<name>/SKILL.md`, and three are also wired as **subagents** under
`.claude/agents/`. Each is placed at the simplest form that does its job.

**Capability-to-form map** — every verb the app must perform, named before any
tool, then matched to the cheapest of the five forms (native action, skill,
subagent, connector, fixed pipeline) that does the job:

| Verb | Form chosen | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Read the raw TBS CSV / PSES microdata | **Native action** | Claude Code already reads a local file well — nothing to build. |
| Validate the dataset against its contract | **Skill** (`edi-data-guard`) | A rule that must hold every run, with a code check that blocks the build. |
| Compute severity, priority, divergence, trends | **Fixed pipeline** (`pipeline/*.py`) | Identical inputs must always give identical outputs — hard-coded, ordered steps. |
| Route a question to Answer / Reframe / Refuse | **Subagent** (`question-router`) | One distinct decision worth its own scoped attention. |
| Classify year-over-year movement | **Subagent** (`trend-interpreter`) | A second distinct decision — is this genuine or benchmark-driven. |
| Render the five-block briefing | **Subagent** (`presentation-summary-render`) | A third distinct decision — which language is defensible here. |
| Enforce cautious language on every answer | **Skill** (`interpretation-guardrails`) | Advisory, not a hard gate — recurs everywhere the app writes a sentence. |
| Reach a live outside system | **Connector: none, deliberately** | The pipeline reads a local/published file and writes a local file; nothing to reach at analysis time. |

**The flow — validate → build → interpret → present.** Each stage names the
capability that governs it:

```
  raw sources            edi-data-guard            build_dataset.py            interpretation-guardrails
  TBS CSV +        ─▶    Skill + validate.py  ─▶   severity · priority   ─▶    trend-interpreter
  PSES EEINFODV          (blocks a bad file)       divergence · trends         question-router
                                                          │                    presentation-summary-render
                                                          ▼                              │
                                                  web/src/data/*.json  ────────▶   dashboard + Ask
                                                                                  (guardrails live)
```

The guard **gates** the data; the pipeline **computes** the derived fields; the
guardrail skills + subagents **shape** every interpretation and answer; the
dashboard and the Ask route are where it **reaches a human**.

| Capability | Form | Where it runs | What it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| `edi-data-guard` | **Skill + runnable check** | build / pipeline gate | `scripts/validate.py` runs 7 checks and **exits non-zero** on a bad dataset, blocking the build. Demonstrable on a deliberately broken CSV. |
| `interpretation-guardrails` | **Skill** | Ask route system prompt; signal logic | Enforces cautious language: no causal claim, no blame, WFA named, bounded action vocabulary, PwD caveat. |
| `question-router` | **Skill + Subagent** | Ask route (runtime); `.claude/agents/question-router.md` | Routes a question to Answer / Reframe / Refuse. Enforced live in `web/src/app/api/ask/route.ts`. |
| `trend-interpreter` | **Skill + Subagent** | dashboard code; `.claude/agents/trend-interpreter.md` | Classifies YoY movement (genuine / benchmark-driven / worsening / stable). Realized deterministically in `web/src/lib/signal.ts`; the subagent is the workspace tool. |
| `presentation-summary-render` | **Skill + Subagent** | Present view; `.claude/agents/presentation-summary-render.md` | The fixed 5-block briefing. Realized in `web/src/views/PresentView.tsx`. |

**Connector:** none, and that is deliberate — the pipeline reads a local/published
file and writes a local data file; there is no live external system to reach at
analysis time. (The Ask route reaches the Claude API, which is the reasoning layer,
not a data connector.)

**The honest runtime picture:** the deployed dashboard is a static Next.js app; its
deterministic logic (severity, priority, trend classification, the 5-block summary)
ships in code. The **Ask route is the live agentic surface** — it calls Claude with
`question-router` + `interpretation-guardrails` in the system prompt and the data as
grounded context. The skills/subagents are the build-time contracts and workspace
tools that produced and govern that behaviour. Nothing in the UI claims a runtime
capability the source does not have.

## 6. Human-in-the-loop & governance

The dashboard is a **signal surface, not a decision engine** — a human makes every
consequential call.
- Priority flags surface *candidates* for review; an EDI policy lead or HR executive
  must review before any action. The UI and the Ask assistant both state this.
- The Ask assistant **refuses** staffing/hiring/firing/individual-promotion requests
  and **reframes** causal questions; it never asserts cause.
- `edi-data-guard` is the data membrane: a bad dataset is blocked before it reaches
  the dashboard.
- Overrides (a dismissed flag, a replaced number) are logged with a reason in
  `Deployment_Log.md`.
- Deployment governance: changes reach the live site only through a reviewed pull
  request to `main` (branch protection); a human owns what crosses into production.

07Data and Context

Sources:

  • Representation & benchmark — Treasury Board Employment Equity data (BT1-28), FY2021-22 through FY2024-25. The input of record is a wide-format government CSV (one row per department per year); pipeline/extract_bt1_28_representation.py reshapes it to long format and computes gap/expected/severity, gated by the edi-data-guard skill. 576 rows = 288 (2023-24) + 288 (2024-25), 72 departments × 4 groups each, 77 suppressed. A separate four-year series feeds the Track view.
  • Employee experience — the 2024 Public Service Employee Survey (PSES), Employment Equity Derived Variable dataset. pipeline/extract_pses.py reads the raw microdata; pipeline/build_dataset.py joins it at the department×equity-group level.

Connection type: file-based, through a reproducible pipeline — not pasted into a prompt and not a live API call at analysis time. The one live connection in the whole system is the Ask route, which calls the Claude API at request time with the pre-computed data as grounded context.

Fields that matter most: representation % (with its raw N), the WFA benchmark, the signed gap (headcount), a severity band, a priority flag (bottom-quartile gap within its own equity-group×year cohort), year-over-year movement, and PSES context (engagement, diversity & inclusion, harassment, discrimination — mobility/retention is honestly omitted because it is structurally suppressed at this breakdown, not fabricated).

Cleaning / transformation: department-name normalization across fiscal years (to catch real trend lines that a naive exact-string join would silently drop), an explicit correction for a source PDF OCR typo found in three fiscal years, and an explicit correction for a department entity named inconsistently across the source's own tables — each documented, not silently guessed.

Limitations: the WFA benchmark is service-wide, not departmental, which makes the ranking a triage signal rather than a precise league table; self-identification is voluntary, so groups — especially Persons with Disabilities — may be undercounted; the WFA benchmark was substantially rebased in 2023-24, so a gap change across that boundary reflects the rebase, not hiring, and the Track view marks this explicitly; per-department subgroup representation (e.g. Black, First Nations, Métis representation by department) does not exist in any available source and is out of scope for that reason, not by oversight.

08The Build

Live dashboard: https://equity-gap-dashboard.vercel.app — a static Next.js 16 / React 19 app deployed on Vercel, auto-deploying from main on every merged pull request.

What the user sees first (Frame, the landing view): how many of the 288 current department×group combinations sit below benchmark, how many are severely below, a severity-distribution bar, and a per-group breakdown — the headline numbers before any filtering.

Dashboard Features

Explore — ranked triage

Shows what it shows: every department×group row for the current year, filterable by group and sortable by gap (headcount), distance below benchmark (pp), or representation rate. What decision signal it provides: which combinations are furthest below benchmark right now, with a priority flag for the bottom quartile within each group. How to interpret it: a high rank means "look here first," refined against departmental context before any conclusion — the benchmark is service-wide, not departmental. Caution: suppressed cells show as suppressed, never as zero.

Compare — cross-cutting patterns and the divergence lens

Shows compounding shortfalls across groups within a department, plus a divergence lens: departments at or above benchmark on representation but whose PSES harassment/discrimination scores sit meaningfully below the same group's public-service-wide average. Signal: representation meeting a benchmark does not necessarily mean the workplace experience is good — two separate signals, never merged into one score. Caution: this is a floor-based flag (≥5pp below peer average), not a statistical claim.

Subgroups — per-department experience detail plus service-wide reference

A department/group picker showing workplace-experience scores (harassment, belonging, career, leadership, workplace, wellbeing) by subgroup, across three survey cycles (2020/2022/2024), from a cross-validated external source; below it, real but service-wide-only reference tables (racialized, Indigenous, and disability subgroups; salary and age distribution; WFA-benchmark history). Signal: within-group divergence (a subgroup reporting notably worse harassment scores than its group's overall figure) and an executive-representation-gap flag. Caution: neither table contains per-department subgroup representation — that data does not exist in any available source.

Track — year-over-year movement with a benchmark-rebase filter

A sparkline of representation vs. benchmark over up to four fiscal years per department×group, classified as genuine improvement, benchmark-driven narrowing, worsening, or stable. A "Compare years" control lets a user pick any two of the four years and see the movement — and the headcount gap — between exactly that pair. Signal: a narrowing gap across the 2023-24 benchmark rebase is never classified as improvement if the benchmark moved, not the hiring. Caution: two data points are not a trend; the label only applies within a single benchmark era.

Present — the five-block briefing generator

Pick a department and group; the app renders a fixed five-block summary (Key Finding, Evidence, Caveat, Human Review Required, Next Action) in cautious, bounded language, ending with a data-attribution line. Signal: a ready-to-brief document for a deputy-head conversation, generated in place of drafting cautious language from scratch each time. Caution: the structure and vocabulary are fixed by the presentation-summary-render skill/subagent — it never varies the block order or introduces unapproved language.

Ask — a real, guardrailed conversational assistant

A live Claude API route, not a keyword matcher. Grounds every figure it cites in the underlying dataset, refuses to produce a composite score across the four designated groups, and refuses or reframes causal and staffing questions rather than answering them directly. Signal: an analyst's own question, answered defensibly. Caution: it will not name individuals, recommend specific hires, or assert a cause for a gap — by design, and independently verified to actually behave this way at runtime (see §11).

Preview — clearly labeled illustrative mockups

Shows what an Executive Pipeline, Workforce Flows, Region-of-work, and Occupational-groups view would look like once real per-department data exists for them, using a source's own placeholder numbers, labeled as concept-only on every section — never presented as a finding.

09Agentic Workspace: Skills, Capabilities, and Subagents

Five capabilities are installed as real Claude Code Skills under .claude/skills/<name>/SKILL.md, three of which are also wired as subagents under .claude/agents/. Each was chosen as the simplest of the course's five capability forms (native action, skill, subagent, connector, fixed pipeline) that does its job — not the most impressive one available.

edi-data-guard Skill + runnable check (hard gate)

Location
.claude/skills/edi-data-guard/ (SKILL.md, scripts/validate.py, references/columns.json)
Input
The representation CSV (or a candidate replacement) before it enters the pipeline
Output
Per-check PASS/WARN/FAIL lines and a process exit code — 0 clean, 1 blocked
What it does
Runs seven checks against the dataset before any dashboard build: required columns present, equity-group labels canonical, numeric fields are clean floats (not "4.1%" as a string), suppressed cells are genuinely blank rather than a hidden zero, the WFA benchmark matches the known service-wide value for that group and year, the gap/expected arithmetic reconciles independently, and departments present in both fiscal years are correctly identified for trend eligibility.
Why it was useful
This is the one government-numbers rule that cannot be left to hope: a wrong column, a substituted benchmark, or a suppressed cell silently read as zero would ship as a "correct" number and misdirect a real oversight review. The rule is written once in references/columns.json and enforced in code on every run, demonstrably — it has actually caught real defects in this project (see §11), not just in a hypothetical broken-file test.
How it contributed
It gates every pipeline run; a bad dataset cannot reach web/src/data/equity.json.

interpretation-guardrails Skill (advisory)

Location
.claude/skills/interpretation-guardrails/SKILL.md
Input
A draft sentence describing a gap, severity, or recommendation
Output
Approved, cautious phrasing, or a rejection with the violated check named
What it does
Enforces five checks on any language the app produces about a gap: no causal claim ("caused by", "proves discrimination"), no blame or moral ranking of a department, the WFA benchmark always named alongside a percentage, recommended actions limited to a fixed, approved vocabulary (recruitment pipeline review, retention review, promotion pipeline review, accessibility review, workplace inclusion review), and a mandatory self-identification caveat whenever Persons with Disabilities is discussed.
Why it was useful
This is the difference between a dashboard that flags a pattern and one that quietly implies a verdict. It is the reason the Ask assistant can be asked a leading, adversarial question and still answer defensibly.
How it contributed
Its rules are the system prompt for the Ask route and the vocabulary baked into web/src/lib/signal.ts's deterministic sentence-generation.

question-router Skill + Subagent

Location
.claude/skills/question-router/SKILL.md, .claude/agents/question-router.md; enforced live in web/src/app/api/ask/route.ts
Input
A free-text question from a user of the Ask tab
Output
One of three dispositions — Answer, Reframe, or Refuse — plus the response text itself
What it does
Decides, before answering, whether a question is in scope (answerable from the approved dataset), whether it requires causal inference (always out of scope, regardless of confidence), and whether the answer is actually computable. A factual question about a specific department's numbers gets Answered, grounded in the data; a "why does this gap exist" question gets Reframed with the factual data plus an explanation of what the data cannot say; a staffing or individual-level question gets Refused outright.
Why it was useful
This is the one place in the app where a user could plausibly phrase a request cleverly enough to extract exactly what the spec forbids — a caused-by claim or a staffing recommendation. Routing the decision to a dedicated subagent, rather than hoping a single system prompt holds under adversarial pressure, is what makes the refusal reliable rather than best-effort.
How it contributed
It is the live agentic surface of the whole project — independently verified at runtime (not just in a spec) to refuse a composite-score request and a causal request when directly provoked (see §11).

trend-interpreter Skill + Subagent

Location
.claude/skills/trend-interpreter/SKILL.md, .claude/agents/trend-interpreter.md; realized deterministically in web/src/lib/signal.ts
Input
A department×group's representation and WFA benchmark at two points in time
Output
One of four classifications (genuine improvement, benchmark-driven narrowing, worsening, stable) plus a one-sentence, non-causal explanation
What it does
Distinguishes a real representation change from a shift in the benchmark itself — the specific judgment a formula alone cannot make, since a narrowing gap can be driven entirely by the benchmark moving rather than by hiring or retention. Never attributes a change to a specific policy or program.
Why it was useful
The 2023-24 WFA rebase (Persons with Disabilities 9.2%→12.0%, Members of Visible Minorities 17.3%→22.7%) made this distinction load-bearing: without it, a rebase-driven gap widening could easily be misread on-screen as the department getting worse at hiring.
How it contributed
Powers the Track view's classification badges and its generalized year-range comparison (any two of the four fiscal years, not just current-vs-prior).

presentation-summary-render Skill + Subagent

Location
.claude/skills/presentation-summary-render/SKILL.md, .claude/agents/presentation-summary-render.md; realized in web/src/views/PresentView.tsx
Input
A chosen department×group's full row of computed fields (representation, WFA, gap, severity, rank, trend)
Output
A fixed five-block briefing, capped at roughly 300 words, ending with a data-attribution line
What it does
Produces the same five blocks — Key Finding, Evidence, Caveat, Human Review Required, Next Action — in the same order, every time, in cautious language. The structure is fixed (a skill's job); choosing what to say within each block for a specific department×group is a judgment call (a subagent's job) — which finding matters most, what the trend means here, what a concrete, bounded next step looks like.
Why it was useful
Turns "draft defensible language for a deputy-head conversation" from a five-minute manual task into a one-click, consistently-worded briefing — directly serving user story #3 in CLAUDE.md.
How it contributed
Powers the entire Present tab.

The reproducible pipeline Fixed pipeline

Location
pipeline/extract_bt1_28_representation.py, pipeline/extract_pses.py, pipeline/build_dataset.py, pipeline/build_history.py, pipeline/build_subgroup_pses.py, pipeline/build_service_wide_context.py
Input
Raw government source files (the BT1-28 CSV, the PSES microdata, a teammate's cross-validated subgroup-experience source)
Output
web/src/data/*.json — every JSON file the dashboard reads
What it does
A hard-coded, ordered sequence of steps — reshape, join, compute severity/priority/divergence/trend, validate, write — that always produces the same output from the same input. Chosen as a fixed pipeline (rather than, say, a subagent) precisely because identical inputs must always give identical outputs for a government dataset; no judgment should enter this stage.
Why it was useful
Every derived field the dashboard shows — severity, priority, divergence, trend classification — is computed once, in code, the same way every time, and is independently re-derivable by eval/run_eval.py. This is what makes the numbers auditable rather than asserted.
How it contributed
It is the entire data layer of the dashboard.

Reading the raw source files Native action

Reading a local CSV or a PSES microdata file is something Claude Code already does well without any package wrapped around it — the cheapest capability is the one that is not built. No skill, subagent, or connector was created for this step.

Reaching a live outside system Connector: none, deliberately

While this project does not contain a connector, the capability that would need one — a live external data or tool connection during analysis — does not exist in this project's design. The pipeline reads a local/published file and writes a local file; there is no live external system to reach at analysis time. The one live call in the whole system is the Ask route calling the Claude API, which is the reasoning layer, not a data connector. This is a documented, deliberate choice in CLAUDE.md §5, not an unexplored gap.

11Evaluation and Governance

Evaluation Purpose

Two independent evaluations were run on this project, each testing the dashboard's output, not its code. The first is a machine-recomputed harness comparing the Day-2 baseline against the Day-3 rebuild. The second, run specifically ahead of this final submission, is a blind, independent governance-critic agent testing the live, currently-deployed app against its own written spec — a different kind of evidence than the harness, aimed at the behaviors a numeric recomputation cannot see (does the Ask assistant actually refuse a leading question; is a suppressed cell actually rendered as suppressed; is the responsible-use caveat actually visible everywhere).

Baseline Evaluation (Day 2 → Day 3 harness)

eval/run_eval.py re-derives representation %, expected-at-benchmark, gap, severity, and the priority quartile independently from raw fields and checks them against a pinned oracle (RCMP · Persons with Disabilities · 2024-25 = 5.5%, N=590 of 10,822, WFA 12.0%, gap −709). At the Day-2 baseline, three of ten checks could not pass: severity and priority were pre-computed offline and not reproducible from the delivered files, and two PSES columns were 100% empty while the documentation claimed five indicators.

Revisions Made

  • Rebuilt severity and priority as pipeline-computed, reproducible fields.
  • Traced the two empty PSES columns to the raw microdata; backfilled discrimination (computable) and documented mobility/retention as genuinely suppressed at this breakdown (not fabricated).
  • Replaced the keyword-matched Ask tab with a real Claude API route carrying the guardrail skills in its system prompt.
  • Normalized department names across fiscal years so real trend lines were not silently dropped by an exact-string join.

After-Revision Evaluation

All ten checks now pass, re-run most recently against the current 576-row, 72-department dataset (expanded from an earlier 35/71-department extract after a later source-relocation episode — see Deployment_Log.md).

Governance and Human Review

  • Where human judgment is still needed: every priority flag surfaces a candidate for review; an EDI policy lead or HR executive must review before any action, exactly as the UI and the Ask assistant both state.
  • What should not be automated: any staffing, hiring, firing, or individual-promotion decision — the Ask assistant is built to refuse these outright, and this was independently verified, not just claimed.
  • Where the dashboard could mislead: the service-wide (not departmental) WFA benchmark can make an operationally-heavy department look worse than a policy shop under the same bar; this is documented explicitly in CLAUDE.md rather than left implicit.
  • What assumptions should be checked: a gap change across the 2023-24 benchmark rebase reflects the rebase, not hiring; self-identification undercounts, especially for Persons with Disabilities.
  • When a user should override the dashboard: whenever departmental context (occupational mix, a known data-quality issue, a recent reorganization) contradicts what the service-wide ranking implies — the dashboard is explicit that its ranking is directional, not exact.
  • How the dashboard supports decisions without replacing the decision-maker: every output ends at a signal — a flag, a caveat, a recommended review type — never a decision, a composite score, or a causal claim.
Honesty note: this project's evidence is strong on two of the three evaluation streams the course describes — the machine harness and the deployment week/blind critic pass. The third stream, a live human pitch reviewed by an outside expert plus five peer teams' cold reads, requires an actual class session and has not happened at the time of writing. It is marked as missing evidence in Deployment_Assessment.md rather than fabricated.

13Limitations

  • Data limitations: the WFA benchmark is service-wide, not departmental, making the ranking directional rather than exact; self-identification undercounts some groups, especially Persons with Disabilities; PSES mobility/retention is structurally suppressed at this breakdown and is honestly omitted; per-department subgroup representation does not exist in any available source.
  • Evaluation limitations: the machine harness (eval/run_eval.py) verifies arithmetic and structural correctness, not narrative quality, which is reviewed by hand instead; the independent governance critic could not test JS-interactive behavior directly (no browser tool was available to it that session), so several claims were closed out by the team's own follow-up checks instead of the critic's — a real gap in independence, disclosed rather than hidden.
  • Deployment limitations: one week of real use produced impressions, not statistics, consistent with the course's own framing of the deployment week; the human evaluation pass (an outside pitch review plus peer cold-reads) has not occurred as of this submission.
  • What the dashboard cannot conclude: it cannot establish why a gap exists, whether discrimination occurred, or which specific employee to hire or promote — by design, and enforced at the Ask route, not merely stated.
  • Future work: a real human critique pass (investor-style pitch plus peer cold-reads); departmental (not just service-wide) WFA benchmarks, if TBS ever publishes them; resolving the Explore route's client-render-only initial state.
